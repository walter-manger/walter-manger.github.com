Without measurement, you are not accomplishing anything (kind of)

For the past couple of weeks I’ve been learning a lot of new things and coding at the seat of my pants as a result of my growing Go coding responsibilities at work. I love learning new things, but there’s just one problem: I can’t really tell you what I’ve learned. All I know is that some feature is shipped and tests were written and people are using it. It’s functional. It’s great that I shipped, but what would put the icing on the cake is if would never have to relearn the things I had to in these past couple of weeks.

During this time period, it appears that I’ve also gotten worse at tracking tasks and the time that I spent on them than I was in the previous weeks.

Getting caught up in the churning

Sometimes while we are working, especially in the software industry, we are rapidly learning things to get something shipped, but we never revisit until the next time a similar task comes our way. Then, it’s off to SO or better to get the same answer we got last time. Sometimes we don’t even recognize that it’s the same problem that we solved last time. We are so focused on finding the answer that we don’t take time to truly understand the problem. We are caught up in churning out the code.

Think about what happens to someone that operates like this full-time for years. That person never progresses past the point of knowing where to get the answer.

It’s not just about work

Slow down to speed up, but how?

Don’t let your inner project manager deprive you from getting better at things. Take the time to learn things from reliable sources. Keep a journal of the things you are working on and what you are learning. Revisit this jounal at the end of each week. The journal does not have to be very detailed, just enough to trigger the same synapse that you had when you discovered the answer.
